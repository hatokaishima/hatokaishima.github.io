passage 1 finding fossil man

1.We can read of things that happened 5,000 years ago in the Near East, where people first learned to write.

  • ‘things that …’: defining relative clause to give essential information about someone or something–information that we need in order to understand what or who is being referred to. ‘that’ is the relative pronoun (= ‘thing’, so we do not repeat ‘things’) and cannot be omitted because it is the subject of the relative clause (while ‘things’ is the object of the main clause), not object, and followed by a verb (‘happened’).
  • ‘where people …’: non-defining relative clause to give extra information about ‘the Near East’ (it isn’t essential for understanding what we are talking about.) , so we use commas (,) to separate it from the rest of the sentence. ‘where’ is the relative adverb and can be replace by ‘which’ with a preposition.

2.But there are some parts of the world where even now people cannot write.

  • ‘some parts of the world where …’: defining relative clause, ‘where’ is the relative pronoun in informal language.

3.The only way that they can preserve their history is to recount it as sagas–legends handed down from one generation of storytellers to another.

  • ‘The only way that …’: defining relative clause, ‘that’ can be left out if it is followed by a subject (‘they’).

4.These legends are useful, because they can tell us something about migrations of people who lived long ago, but none could write down what they did.

  • ‘people who …’: defining relative clause.
  • ‘write down what …’: defining relative clause, ‘what’ = ‘the things that’.

5.Anthropologists wondered where the remote ancestors of the Polynesian peoples now living in the Pacific Islands came from.

  • ‘wondered where …’: defining relative clause.
  • wonder: to think about something and try to decide what is true, what will happen, what you should do, etc.
  • ‘peoples now living in …’ = ‘peoples now who is living in …’. Past participle clause. Participle clauses enable us to say information in a more economical way. They are formed using present participles (going, reading, seeing, walking, etc.), past participles (gone, read, seen, walked, etc.) or perfect participles (having gone, having read, having seen, having walked, etc.). We can use participle clauses when the participle and the verb in the main clause have the same subject. Participle clauses do not have a specific tense. The tense is indicated by the verb in the main clause. Participle clauses are mainly used in written texts, particularly in a literary, academic or journalistic style.

6.The sagas of these people explain that some of them came from Indonesia about 2,000 years ago.

  • ‘explain that …’: defining relative clause.

7.But the first people who were like ourselves lived so long ago that even their sagas, if they had any, are forgotten.

  • ‘people who …’: defining relative clause.

8.So archaeologists have neither history nor legends to help them to find out where the first ‘modern men’ came from.

  • ‘find out where …’: defining relative clause.

9.Fortunately, however, ancient men made tools of stone, especially flint, because this is easier to shape than other kinds.

  • made of: the basic material or qualities of something.
  • made from: how something is manufactured, ‘Plastic is made from oil.’
  • made out of: something that has been changed or transformed from one thing into another, ‘candle-holders made out of wine bottles’.
  • made with: the ingredients of food and drink, ‘sushi always made with raw fish’.

10.so the tools of long ago have remained when even the bones of the men who made them have disappeared without trace.

  • ‘the men who …’: defining relative clause.

passage 2 spare that spider

1.Why, you may wonder, should spiders be our friends?

  • wonder: to be very surprised by something.

2.Because they destroy so many insects, and insects include some of the greatest enemies of the human race.

  • destroy: to damage something so badly that it no longer exists, works, etc.
  • the human race: [singular] all people, considered together as a group.

3.if it were not for the protection we get from insect-eating animals.

  • ‘if it were …’: imagined condition to talk about the possible result of an imagined situation in the present or future that are impossible or unlikely in reality. When if is followed by the verb be, it is grammatically correct to say if I were, if he were, if she were and if it were. However, it is also common to hear these structures with was, especially in the he/she form.

4.We owe a lot to the birds and beasts who eat insects, but all of them put together kill only a fraction of the number destroyed by spiders.

  • put together: (used after a noun or nouns referring to a group of people or things) combined.

5.Moreover, unlike some of the other insect eaters, spiders never do the least harm to us and our belongs.

  • do the least harm to: do … harm to …, harm is noun here.

6.Spiders are not insects, as many people think, nor even nearly related to them.

  • ‘related to them’: relate to something/somebody, to be connected with something/somebody; to refer to something/somebody.

7.One can tell the difference almost at a glance, for a spider always has eight legs and an insect never more than six.

  • ‘tell the difference’: tell, [transitive] (not used in the progressive tenses or in the passive) to recognize the difference between one thing or person and another.

8.How many spiders are engaged in this work on our behalf?

  • ‘are engaged in this work’: engage in something | engage somebody in something, (formal) to take part in something; to make somebody take part in something.
  • ‘on our behalf’: on behalf of somebody | on somebody’s behalf, because of somebody; for somebody.

9.One authority on spiders made a census of the spiders in a grass field in the south of England, and he estimated there were more than 2,250,000 in one acre; that is something like 6,000,000 spiders of different kinds on a football pitch.

  • ‘authority on spiders’: authority (on something) a person with special knowledge.
  • ‘that is …’: ‘that’ = ‘he estimated there were more than 2,250,000 in one acre’, relative clauses refer to a whole sentence or a longer stretch of language, always use which to introduce these clauses in informal speaking to express an opinion or evaluation.

10.It is impossible to make more than the wildest guess at how many they kill, but they are hungry creatures, not content with only three meals a day.

  • ‘make more than the wildest guess’: wild, having no discipline or control. ‘beyond somebody’s wildest dreams’: far more, better, etc. than you could ever have imagined or hoped for.

passage 3 matterhorn man

1.Modern alpinists try to climb mountains by a route which will give them good sport, and the more difficult it is, the more highly it is regarded.

  • sport: [uncountable] (old-fashioned) pleasure or fun.

2.The early climbers were looking for the easiest way to the top, because the summit was the prize they sought, especially if it had never been attained before.

  • attain: to succeed in getting something, usually after a lot of effort.

3.It is true that during their explorations they often faced difficulties and dangers of the most perilous nature, equipped in a manner which would make a modern climber shudder at the thought, but they did not go out of their way to court such excitement.

  • perilous: (formal or literary) very dangerous.
  • shudder: [intransitive] to shake because you are cold or frightened, or because of a strong feeling.
  • thought: [countable] something that you think of or remember, at the thought of something.
  • ‘go out of their way’: go out of one’s way (to do sth), take particular care and trouble to do sth.
  • court: [transitive] (formal) to try to obtain something.
  • ‘equipped in a manner …’: Past participle clause.

4.They had a single aim, a solitary goal–the top!

  • solitary: [usually before noun] (especially in negative sentences and questions) only one.

5.It is hard for us to realize nowadays how difficult it was for the pioneers. Except for one or two places such as Zermatt and Chamonix, which had rapidly become popular, Alpine villages tended to be impoverished settlements cut off from civilization by the high mountains.

  • realize: (not used in the progressive tenses) to understand or become aware of a particular fact or situation.
  • impoverished: very poor; without money.
  • ‘cut off from civilization by …’: cut somebody/something off (from somebody/something), [often passive] to prevent somebody/something from leaving or reaching a place or communicating with people outside a place.

6.Such inns as there were were generally dirty and flea-ridden; the food simply local cheese accompanied by bread often twelve months old, all washed down with coarse wine.

  • washed down with: to drink something after, or at the same time as, eating something.
  • coarse: (of food, wine, etc) of low quality; inferior.

7.Often a valley boasted no inn at all, and climbers found shelter wherever they could–sometimes with the local priest (who was usually as poor as his parishioners), sometimes with shepherds or cheese-makers.

  • boast: [transitive] (not used in the progressive tenses) to have something that is impressive.
  • shelter: [uncountable] protection from rain, danger, or attack.

8.For men accustomed to eating seven-course dinners and sleeping between fine linen sheets at home, the changes to the Alps must have been very hard indeed.

  • must: modal verb, used to say that something is likely or logical.
  • ‘… must have been very hard indeed’: We can use modal verbs for deduction – guessing if something is true using the available information. The modal verb we choose shows how certain we are about the possibility. We use must have + past participle when we feel sure about what happened.

passage 4 seeing hands

1.In the Soviet Union several cases have been reported recently of people who can read can detect colours with their finger, and even see through solid doors and walls.

  • detect: to discover or notice something, especially something that is not easy to see, hear, etc.
  • solid: having no holes or spaces inside; not hollow.

2.One case concerns an eleven-year-old schoolgirl, Vera Petrova, who has normal vision but who can also perceive things with different parts of her skin, and through solid walls.

  • concern: (also be concerned with something) to be about something.
  • perceive: to notice or become aware of something.

3.One day, she came into his office and happened to put her hands on a locked safe.

  • ‘happened to put …’: happen to be/do something: to do or be something by chance.

4.Suddenly she asked her father why he kept so many old newspapers locked away there, and even described the way they were done up in bundles.

  • ‘locked away …’: lock something away (also lock something up), to put something in a safe place that can be locked.
  • ‘done up in bundles’: to make something into a package.

5.Vera’s curious talent was brought to the notice of a scientific research institute in the town of Ulyanovsk, near where she lives, and in April she was given a series of tests by a special commission of the Ministry of Health of the Russian Federal Republic.

  • curious: strange and unusual.
  • ‘brought to the notice of …’: notice, [uncountable] the fact of somebody paying attention to somebody/something or knowing about something. bring sth to sb’s notice, (fml) tell sb about sth, show sb sth, etc.
  • commission: [countable] an official group of people who have been given responsibility to control something, or to find out about something, usually for the government. committee: a group of people who are chosen, usually by a larger group, to make decisions or to deal with a particular subject.

6.During these tests she was able to read a newspaper through an opaque screen, and stranger still, by moving her elbow over a child’s game of Lotto she was able to describe the figures and the colours printed on it; and in another instance, wearing stockings and slippers, to make out with her foot the outlines and colours of a picture hidden under a carpet.

  • screen: [countable] a tall, flat piece of furniture or equipment that is fixed to a wall, etc. or that can be moved to divide a room or to keep one area hidden or separate.
  • ‘make out …’: to manage to see somebody/something or read or hear something.

7.During all these tests Vera was blindfold; and, indeed, except when blindfold she lacked the ability to perceive things with her skin.

  • blindfold: to cover somebody’s eyes with a piece of cloth or other material so that they cannot see. It is an adjective (with the eyes blindfolded) here.

passage 6 youth

1.People are always talking about ‘the problem of youth’.

  • ‘are always talking …’: if you say a person is always doing something, or something is always happening, you mean that they do it, or it happens, very often, and that this is annoying.

2.If there is one–which I take leave to doubt–then it is old people who create it, not the young themselves.

  • take leave to doubt: beg leave to do something (formal) to ask somebody for permission to do something.

3.Let us get down to fundamentals and agree that the young are after all human beings–people just like their elders.

  • elders: [plural] people of greater age, experience and authority.

4.There is only one difference between an old man and a young one: the young man has glorious future before him, and the old one has a splendid future behind him: and maybe that is where the rub is.

  • glorious: (formal) deserving or bringing great success and making somebody/something famous.
  • splendid: (especially British English) very impressive; very beautiful.
  • future: [singular, uncountable] the possibility of being successful or surviving at a later time.
  • the rub: [singular] (formal or humorous) a problem or difficulty.

5.When I was a teenage, I felt that I was just young and uncertain–that I was a new boy in a huge school, and I would have been very pleased to be regarded as something so interesting as a problem.

  • uncertain: likely to change, especially in a negative or unpleasant way.

6.For one thing, being a problem gives you a certain identity, and that is one of the things the young are busily engaged in seeking.

  • For one thing: used to introduce a reason for sth.

7.I find young people exciting.

  • find: [transitive] to have a particular feeling or opinion about something.
  • exciting: causing great interest or excitement.

8.They have an air of freedom, and they have not a dreary commitment to mean ambitions or love of comfort.

  • ‘an air of …’: air, [singular] the particular feeling or impression that is given by somebody/something; the way somebody does something.
  • freedom: the power or right to do or say what you want without anyone stopping you.
  • dreary: that makes you feel sad; not bright or interesting.
  • commitment: [uncountable] the desire to work hard and give your energy and time to a job or an activity.
  • mean: (old-fashioned) born into or coming from a low social class.
  • ambitions: [countable] something that you want to do or achieve very much.
  • comfort: [uncountable] the state of being physically relaxed and free from pain; the state of having a pleasant life, with everything that you need.

9.They are not anxious social climbers, and they have no devotion to material things.

  • devotion: [uncountable, singular] devotion (to somebody/something) the action of spending a lot of time or energy on something.
  • material: [only before noun] connected with money, possessions, etc. rather than with the needs of the mind or spirit.

10.All this seems to me to link them with life, and the origins of things.

  • life: [uncountable] the quality of being active and exciting.

11.It’s as if they were, in some sense, cosmic beings in violent and lovely contrast with us suburban creatures.

  • ‘as if they were …’: We use as if and as though to make comparisons. They have a similar meaning. We use as if and as though to talk about an imaginary situation or a situation that may not be true but that is likely or possible. As if is more common than as though. We can use both as if and as though followed by a non-finite clause or a prepositional phrase. As if and as though commonly follow the verbs feel and look. In informal English, like can be used in a similar way to as if, though it is not always considered correct in formal contexts.
  • cosmic: connected with the whole universe.
  • violent: [usually before noun] very strong and sudden.
  • lovely: (informal) very pleasant; wonderful.
  • suburban: (disapproving) boring and ordinary.

12.He may be conceited, ill-mannered, presumptuous or fatuous, but I do not turn for protection to dreary cliches about respect for elders–as if mere age were a reason for respect.

  • conceited: (disapproving) being too proud of yourself and what you do.
  • presumptuous: [not usually before noun] too confident, in a way that shows a lack of respect for other people.
  • fatuous: (formal) stupid.

passage 7 the sporting spirit

1.I am always amazed when I hear people saying that sport creates goodwill between the nations, and that if only the common peoples of the world could meet one another at the football or cricket, they would have no inclinations to meet on the battlefield.

  • sport: [uncountable] (British English) (North American English sports [plural]) activity that you do for pleasure and that needs physical effort or skill, usually done in a special area and according to fixed rules.

2.Even if one didn’t know from concrete examples (the 1936 Olympic Games, for instance) that international sporting contests lead to orgies of hatred, one could deduce it from general principles.

  • concrete: based on facts, not on ideas or guesses.
  • sporting: adj, [only before noun] connected with sports.
  • ‘orgies of …’: )(disapproving) an extreme amount of a particular activity.

3.Nearly all the sports practised nowadays are competitive.

  • sports: [countable] a particular form of sport.

4.On the village green, where you pick up sides and no feeling of local patriotism is involved, it is possible to play simply for the fun and exercise: but as soon as the question of prestige arises, as soon as you feel that you and some large unit will be disgraced if you lose, the most savage combative instincts are aroused.

  • arise: [intransitive] (rather formal) (especially of a problem or a difficult situation) to happen; to start to exist.
  • arouse: to make somebody have a particular feeling or attitude.

5.At the international level, sport is frankly mimic warfare.

  • frankly: in an honest and direct way that people might not like.

6.But the significant thing is not the behaviour of the players but the attitude of the spectators: and behind the spectators, of the nations who work themselves into furies over these absurd contests, and seriously believe–at any rate for short periods–that running, jumping and kicking a ball are tests of national virtue.

  • ‘work themselves into furies …’: work sb/oneself up (into sth), rouse sb/oneself to a state of excitement.
  • at any rate: used to show that you are being more accurate about something that you have just said.

passage 9 bats

1.Not all sounds made by animals serve as language …

  • ‘serve as …’: to be suitable for a particular use, especially when nothing else is available.

2.The further off this solid obstruction, the longer time will elapse for the return of the echo.

  • elapse: if a period of time elapses, it passes. lapse: to gradually become weaker or come to an end.

passage 11 royal espionage

1.Alfred the Great acted as his own spy, visiting Danish camps disguised as a minstrel.

  • ‘acted as …’: [intransitive] to perform a particular role or function.

2.Alfred had learned many of their ballads in his youth, and could vary his programme with acrobatic tricks and simple conjuring.

  • programme: an organized order of performances or events.

3.While Alfred’s little army slowly began to gather at Athelney, the king himself set out to penetrate the camp of Guthrum, the commander of the Danish invaders.

  • penetrate: to succeed in entering or joining an organization, a group, etc. especially when this is difficult to do.

4.These had settled down for the winter at Chippenham: thither Alfred went.

  • ‘settled down’: to start to have a quieter way of life, living in one place.

5.He noticed at once that discipline was slack: the Danes had the self-confidence of conquerors, and their security precautions were casual.

  • slack: (disapproving) not putting enough care, attention or energy into something and so not doing it well enough.
  • casual: [usually before noun] not showing much care or thought; seeming not to be worried; not wanting to show that something is important to you.

6.They lived well, on the proceeds of raids on neighbouring regions. There they collected women as well as food and drink, and a life of ease had made them soft.

  • proceeds: [plural] the money that you receive when you sell something or organize a performance, etc.; profits.
  • raid: an attack on a building, etc. in order to commit a crime.
  • ease: the state of feeling relaxed or comfortable without worries, problems or pain.
  • soft: (informal, disapproving) not brave enough; wanting to be safe and comfortable.

7.The force there assembled was trivial compared with the Danish horde.

  • trivial : not important or serious; not worth considering.
  • horde: (sometimes disapproving) a large crowd of people.

8.So, faced with the Danish advance, Alfred did not risk open battle but harried the enemy.

  • ‘faced with …’: if you face a particular situation, or it faces you, you have to deal with it.
  • harry: to make repeated attacks on an enemy.

9.He was constantly on the move, drawing the Danes after him.

  • on the move: moving.

10.The episode could reasonably serve as a unique epic of royal espionage!

  • serve as: to have a particular effect or result.

passage 14 how to grow old

1.Some old people are oppressed by the fear of death.

  • oppress: to make somebody only able to think about things that make them sad or worried.

2.In the young there is a justification for this feeling.

  • ‘justification for …’: a good reason why something exists or is done.

3.Young men who have reason to fear that they will be killed in battle may justifiably feel bitter in the thought that they have been cheated of the best things that life has to offer.

  • justifiably: for a good reason, and therefore in a way that is acceptable .
  • ‘cheated of …’: cheat somebody of something | cheat somebody out of something, to prevent somebody from having something, especially in a way that is not honest or fair.

4.But in an old man who has known human joys and sorrows, and has achieved whatever work it was in him to do, the fear of death is somewhat abject and ignoble.

  • ‘in an old man’: in, used to show a state or condition, eg, a man in his thirties.
  • ‘has achieved whatever work it was in him to do’ = has achieved it was in him to do whatever work, whatever work he can do. Colossians 1:16 of Bible (New International Version) : For in him all things were created: things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or powers or rulers or authorities; all things have been created through him and for him.

5.I should wish to die while still at work, knowing others will carry on what I can no longer do, and content in the thought that what was possible has been done.

  • should: used for expressing strong agreement.

passage 15 banks and their customers

1.When anyone opens a current account at a bank, he is lending the bank money, repayment of which he may demand at any time, either in cash or by drawing a cheque in favour of another person.

  • ‘in favour of another person’: in favour of sb/sth, (of cheques) payable to (the account of) sb/sth.

2.Many of these obligations can give rise to problems and complications but a bank customer, unlike, say, a buyer of goods, cannot complain that the law is loaded against him.

  • give rise to: (fml) cause sth.
  • ‘loaded against’: load the dice (against/in favour of somebody), to put somebody at an unfair disadvantage/advantage.

3.When, for example, a customer first opens an account, he instructs the bank to debit his account only in respect of cheques drawn by himself.

  • ‘in respect of cheques’: in respect of something (formal or business), in payment for something.

4.He gives the bank specimens of his signature, and there is a very firm rule that the bank has no right or authority to pay out a customer’s money on a cheque on which its customer’s signature has been forged.

  • ‘pay out’: to pay a large sum of money for something.

5.It makes no difference that the forgery may have been a very skilful one: the bank must recognize its customer’s signature.

  • skilful: (US English skillful), made or done very well.

6.For this reason there is no risk to the customer in the modern practice, adopted by some banks, of printing the customer’s name on his cheques.

  • practice: a way of doing something that is the usual or expected way in a particular organization or situation.

7.If this facilitates forgery, it is the bank which will lose, not the customer.

  • ‘it is the bank which will lose’: cleft sentence.

We use cleft sentences, especially in speaking, to connect what is already understood to what is new to the listener. In a cleft sentence, a single message is divided (cleft) into two clauses. This allows us to focus on the new information.

It-clauses are the most common type of cleft clause. The information that comes after it is emphasised for the listener. The clause which follows the it-clause is connected using that and it contains information that is already understood. We often omit that in informal situations when it is the object of the verb.

When a personal subject is the focus, we can use who instead of that. We often omit who in informal situations when it is the object of the verb.

When a plural subject is the focus, we use a plural verb but It + be remains singular.

We can use negative structures in the it-clause.

Wh-cleft sentences are most often introduced by what, but we can also use why, where, how, etc. The information in the wh-clause is typically old or understood information.

passage 16 the search for oil

1.My particular experience is largely in oil, and the search for oil has done more to improve deep drilling than any other mining activity.

  • particular: [only before noun] used to emphasize that you are referring to one individual person, thing or type of thing and not others.
  • largely: to a great extent; mostly or mainly.

2.When it has been decided where we are going to drill, we put up at the surface an oil derrick.

  • it = where we are going to drill.

3.The geologist needs to know what rocks the drill has reached, so every so often a sample is obtained with a coring bit.

  • every so often: occasionally; from time to time.

4.It cuts a clean cylinder of rock, from which can be seen the strata the drill has been cutting through.

  • clean: having a smooth edge, surface or shape; simple and regular.

5.This pressure must be under control, and we control it by means of the mud which we circulate down the drill pipe.

  • ‘by means of …’: means, the plural is also means. by means of sth, (formal) with the help of something.

6.We endeavour to avoid the old, romantic idea of a gusher, which wastes oil and gas.

  • romantic: having an attitude to life where imagination and the emotions are especially important; not looking at situations in a realistic way.

passage 18 secrecy in industry

1.Two factors weigh heavily against the effectiveness of scientific research in industry.

  • research: [uncountable]. The plural form researches is also sometimes used in British English, but is much less frequent.

2.In so far as any inquiry is a secret one, it naturally limits all those engaged in carrying it out from effective contact with their fellow scientists either in other countries or in universities, or even, often enough, in the other departments of the same firm.

  • in so far as: to the extent that.

3.Many firms, for instance, have great difficulties in obtaining technical or scientific books from libraries because they are unwilling to have their name entered as having taken out such and such a book, for fear the agents of other firms should be able to trace the kind of research they are likely to be undertaking.

  • enter: [transitive] to put names, numbers, details, etc. in a list, book or computer.
  • agent: (also secret agent) a person who works secretly to obtain information for a government or other organization. agency: a business or an organization that provides a particular service especially when representing other businesses or organizations.
    should: used to say that you expect something is true or will happen.

passage 20 the modern city

1.In the organization of industrial life the influence of the factory upon the physiological and mental state of the workers has been completely neglected.

  • physiological: connected with the way in which a particular living thing functions.

2.The great cities have been built with no regard for us.

  • ‘regard for …’: [uncountable] (formal) attention to or thought and care for somebody/something.

3.This caused the construction of gigantic buildings where too large masses of human beings are crowded together.

  • ‘masses of …’: [plural] (informal) a large number or amount of something.

4.While they enjoy the comfort and banal luxury of their dwelling, they do not realize that they are deprived of the necessities of life.

  • necessities: [countable] a thing that you must have and cannot manage without.

5.The modern city consists of monstrous edifices and dark, narrow streets full of petrol fumes, coal dust and toxic gases, torn by the noise of taxicabs, lorries and buses, and thronged ceaselessly by great crowds.

  • throng: (literary) to go somewhere or be present somewhere in large numbers.

passage 21 a man-made disease

1.In the early days of the settlement of Australia, enterprising settlers unwisely introduced the European rabbit.

  • settlement: [uncountable] the process of people making their homes in a place.
  • enterprising: (approving) having or showing the ability to think of new projects or new ways of doing things and make them successful.

2.This rabbit had no natural enemies in the Antipodes, so that it multiplied with that promiscuous abandon characteristic of rabbits.

  • the Antipodes: [plural]
  • a way of referring to Australia and New Zealand, often used in a humorous way.

3.It caused devastation by burrowing and by devouring the herbage which might have maintained millions of sheep and cattle.

  • devastation: [uncountable]
  • great destruction or damage, especially over a wide area.
  • herbage: [uncountable] (specialist) plants in general, especially grass that is grown for cows, etc. to eat.

4.By infecting animals and letting them loose in the burrows, local epidemics of this disease could be created.

  • loose: [not usually before noun] free to move around without control; not tied up or shut in somewhere.

5.So while the rest of the world was trying to get rid of mosquitoes, Australia was encouraging this one.

  • encourage: to make something more likely to happen or develop.

6.It later became apparent that rabbits were developing a degree of resistance to this disease, so that the rabbit population was unlikely to be completely exterminated.

  • exterminate: to kill all the members of a group of people or animals.

7.Ironically, Europe, which had bequeathed the rabbit as a pest to Australia, acquired this man-made disease as a pestilence.

  • bequeath: bequeath something (to somebody) | bequeath somebody something to leave the results of your work, knowledge, etc. for other people to use or deal with, especially after you have died.

8.A French physician decided to get rid of the wild rabbits on his own estate and introduced myxomatosis.

  • estate: [countable] a large area of land, usually in the country, that is owned by one person or family.

9.It did not, however, remain within the confines of his own estate.

3.Such a messenger, receiving our radio and television signals, might well re-transmit them back to its home-planet, although what impression any other civilization would thus get from us is best left unsaid.

  • thus: as a result of something just mentioned.

4.But here we come up against the most difficult of all obstacles to contact with people on other planets–the astronomical distances which separate us.

  • come up against: [no passive] to be faced with or opposed by somebody/something.

passage 55 patterns of culture

1.Custom has not been commonly regarded as a subject of any great moment.

  • of any great moment: of moment, very important.

2.The inner workings of our own brains we feel to be uniquely worthy of investigation, but custom, we have a way of thinking, is behaviour at its most commonplace.

  • workings of …: [countable, usually plural] working (of something) the way in which a machine, a system, an organization, etc. works.
  • we have a way of thinking: to my way of thinking, in my opinion.

3.Traditional custom, taken the world over, is a mass of detailed behaviour more astonishing than what any one person can ever evolve in individual actions, no matter how aberrant.

  • taken the world over: take, [transitive] (not used in the progressive tenses) to understand or consider something in a particular way. the world over, the (whole) world over, everywhere in the world.

4.When one seriously studies the social orders that have had the opportunity to develop autonomously, the figure becomes no more than an exact and matter-of-fact observation.

  • matter-of-fact: without showing any emotion, especially in a situation in which you would expect somebody to express their feelings.

passage 57 of men and galaxies

1.Yet their present development is wholly different, not so much because of different people even, but because of different thoughts that exist in minds of their inhabitants.

  • not so much: used to refer back to something that has been mentioned, saying that it is less true for someone or something else.

2.The Holy Office knew this full well when it caused heretics to be burned in days gone by.

  • full: adverb, very.

3.It is only someone looking on from outside that can inject the dangerous thoughts.

  • look on: to watch something without becoming involved in it yourself.

4.Imagine the effect that on a reasonably advanced technological society, one that still does not possess the bomb, of making it aware of the possibility, of supplying sufficient details to enable the thing to be constructed.

  • reasonably: o a degree that is fairly good but not very good.

passage 58 hobbies

1.A gifted American psychologist has said, ‘Worry is a spasm of the emotion; the mind catches hold of something and will not let it go. ’

  • spasm of: a sudden strong feeling or reaction that lasts for a short time.

2.One can only gently insinuate something else into its convulsive grasp.

  • grasp: a strong hold of somebody/something or control over somebody/something, synonym grip. grip: [countable, usually singular] grip (on somebody/something) an act of holding somebody/something tightly; a particular way of doing this.

3.And if this something else is rightly chosen, if it is really attended by the illumination of another field of interest, gradually, and often quite swiftly, the old undue grip relaxes and the process of recuperation and repair begins.

  • attend: (formal) to be with somebody and help them.
  • illumination: [uncountable] (formal) understanding or explanation of something.

4.The cultivation of a hobby and new forms of interest is therefore a policy of the first importance to a public man.

  • public man: public figure (= a person who is well known because they are often on the television, radio, etc.).

5.It is no use offering the manual labourer, tired out with a hard week’s sweat and effort, the chance of playing a game of football or baseball on Saturday afternoon.

  • labourer: a person whose job involves hard physical work that does not need special skills, especially work that is done outdoors.
  • sweat: [uncountable] (informal) hard work or effort.
  • effort: the physical or mental energy that you need to do something; something that takes a lot of energy.

6.It is no use inviting the politician or the professional or business man, who has been working or worrying about serious things for six days, to work or worry about trifling things at weekend.

  • professional: a person who does a job that needs special training and a high level of education.

7.In vain they rush frantically round from place to place, trying to escape from avenging boredom by mere clatter and motion.

  • frantically: without controlling your emotions because you are extremely frightened or worried about something.

8.It may also be said that rational, industrious, useful human beings are divided into two classes: first, those whose work is work and whose pleasure is pleasure; and secondly, those whose work and pleasure are one.

  • industrious: (approving) working hard; busy. industrial: connected with industry.

9.Each day is a holiday, and ordinary holidays, when they come, are grudged as enforced interruptions in an absorbing vocation.

  • vocation: [countable] a type of work or way of life that you believe is especially suitable for you. vacation: (North American English) (British English holiday) [uncountable, countable] a period of time spent travelling or resting away from home.

passage 59 the great escape

1.Economy is one powerful motive for camping, since after the initial outlay upon equipment, or through hiring it, the total expense can be far less than the cost of hotels.

  • economy: the use of resources in a way that avoids waste.

2.That the equipment of modern camping becomes yearly more sophisticated is an entertaining paradox for the cynic, a brighter promise for the hopeful traveller who has sworn to get away from it all.

  • promise: [uncountable] a sign that somebody/something will be successful.

3.It also provides–and some student sociologist who might care to base his thesis upon the phenomenon–an escape of another kind.

  • care to do something: to make the effort to do something.

4.The contemporary phenomenon of motorcar worship is to be explained not least the sense of independence and freedom the ownership entails.

  • not least: especially.

5.Not only are the means of arriving at the holiday paradise entirely within one’s own command and keeping, but the means of escape from holiday hell (if the beach proves too crowded, the local weather is inclement) are there, outside–or, as likely, part of–the tent.

  • keeping: in somebody’s keeping, being taken care of by somebody.

6.The opinion does not survive experience of a popular Continental camping place.

  • survive: to continue to live or exist despite a dangerous event or time.

7…. but as yet there is no overwhelming specialized patronage.

  • as yet: until now or until a particular time in the past.
